I'm A Dietitian. I Read Every Label. I Still Couldn't Figure Out Why My Dog's Health Was Falling Short.

I know more about food science than most people will ever want to. It still took me three years to figure out why none of it was working for my dog — because the answer wasn't on any label.
🥗
By Maya Chen, RD
Registered Dietitian · Dog Mom · Nutrition Researcher
IMAGE: Maya with her dog — professional setting, warm. Dog looks healthy but not obviously thriving.
Caption: "Three years of doing everything right. The results told a different story."

I spend my days telling people how to read food labels. How processing affects nutrient bioavailability. Why ingredient quality matters but isn't the whole story. I've been doing this for eleven years. I've sat across from patients who were eating what looked like a perfect diet and still couldn't understand why they weren't feeling better.

I thought I understood nutrition. Then I got a dog.

His name is Mochi. Shih Tzu mix, four years old, terminally enthusiastic about food. From the beginning, I applied everything I knew. I read the labels. I avoided the fillers and the vague protein sources. I went premium — then more premium — and when the fresh-cooked delivery services started showing up, I was first in line. If there was a "right" way to feed a dog, I was doing it.

For three years, Mochi had soft stools. Not sick-soft — just never right. His coat was fine. Not the kind of fine that makes people stop you on the street, just… adequate. He had less energy at three than he'd had at one, and every vet visit came back clean. "Some dogs just have sensitive stomachs," they'd say. I heard that enough times that I started to believe it.

I was a dietitian blaming genetics for a nutrition problem. It took me an embarrassingly long time to realize that's exactly what I was doing.

"I was applying human nutrition principles to my dog's food. The labels looked right. The ingredients checked out. What I didn't understand was that the label was never the issue — and nothing in my training had prepared me to see that."

What I Got Wrong — And Why Almost Every Dog Owner Gets It Wrong Too

The instinct, when a dog has persistent digestive issues, is to go after the ingredients. Different protein. Fewer additives. Simpler formula. Higher quality sourcing. That's the human nutrition playbook and it's not wrong — ingredient quality matters. But it's incomplete in a way I didn't understand for longer than I should have.

The gap isn't in what goes into the food. It's in what the food does to the gut once it gets there. And that's determined almost entirely by something you can't read on any label: what the processing did to the living components before the bag was sealed.

Here's what I've learned working in nutrition: a functional gut needs ongoing inputs that most people never think about. Beneficial bacteria to populate and maintain the microbiome. Enzymes to help break food down and actually absorb it. And the compounds that keep the gut lining intact — because a compromised gut lining means nutrients pass through without being absorbed, inflammation runs higher than it should, and everything downstream is slightly off. In whole, unprocessed food, these exist naturally. The moment you apply heat to make food shelf-stable, they're gone. Not reduced. Gone.

Kibble Cooked at 250–300°F+. Beneficial bacteria, digestive enzymes, and gut-supporting compounds destroyed before the bag is sealed. Every bag. Even the $90 one with the clean ingredient panel.
"Gently cooked" fresh food Still reaches food-safe temperatures. Still crosses the damage threshold. "Gentle" is a marketing description, not a temperature specification. Better sourcing. Same gap in what the gut actually receives.
Freeze-dried raw No heat. Moisture removed at sub-zero temperatures in a vacuum. The bacteria, enzymes, and bioactive compounds survive intact. What's in the food is what arrives in the gut.

I had moved from kibble to premium kibble to fresh-cooked delivery over the course of three years. Every upgrade improved the label. None of them changed the temperature problem. I was solving the wrong variable, over and over, with increasing sophistication.

The Part That Most Brands — Including The Good Ones — Still Miss

When I finally understood the processing issue, I thought the fix was straightforward: switch to freeze-dried, add a probiotic, done. I'd been recommending probiotics to patients for years. I knew how they worked. I figured I was most of the way there.

I was still missing something.

The research on gut health — in humans and increasingly in dogs — has moved significantly past the probiotic conversation in the last decade. Probiotics matter. But they're the middle of a three-part system, not the whole thing. And without the other two parts, they can't do what most people think they're doing.

The Three-Part Gut System — Why Probiotics Alone Aren't Enough
🌱
Prebiotics — The Foundation
Prebiotics are the food source that beneficial bacteria need to survive and multiply. Without adequate prebiotic input, even a high-quality probiotic supplement has nothing to grow on. The bacteria arrive, find an inhospitable environment, and die off before they can establish. Most dog foods — including many with added probiotics — do not include the prebiotic substrate those probiotics need to function.
🦠
Probiotics — The Population
With a prebiotic foundation in place, probiotic bacteria can actually establish themselves and begin diversifying the microbiome. This is where most gut health products stop. But a populated microbiome is not a functional gut lining — and the gut lining is where most of the downstream benefits actually originate.
⚡
Postbiotics — The Moat
Postbiotics are what beneficial gut bacteria actually produce once they're established and fed — the compounds that do the real work. The most important one is called butyrate, and the simplest way I can describe it is this: it's the fuel that keeps the gut lining sealed. A sealed gut lining means better nutrient absorption, lower inflammation, and the visible results most owners are actually chasing — better coat, better energy, stronger immunity. Without postbiotics, you have a microbiome that exists on paper but isn't producing what the body needs to run well. This is the piece almost every product on the market — probiotic supplements, fresh food, even most freeze-dried brands — leaves out entirely.

When I mapped this out, the three years of Mochi's digestive issues made complete sense. He was getting food. He was occasionally getting probiotics. "Adding a probiotic to a dog's diet without prebiotics and postbiotics is like hiring staff for a building you haven't finished constructing. The people show up. There's nowhere for them to work. Nothing gets done."
